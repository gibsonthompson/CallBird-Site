If your practice handles protected health information (PHI) over the phone, you need a HIPAA-compliant answering service. But here's what most articles won't tell you: the majority of inbound calls to medical offices don't actually involve PHI. Appointment scheduling, directions, hours, insurance questions, and new patient inquiries are all non-PHI conversations that any AI receptionist can handle.

Understanding this distinction can save you hundreds per month by using a general AI receptionist for routine calls while reserving HIPAA-compliant services for calls that genuinely involve PHI.

What is HIPAA Compliance for Phone Answering?

HIPAA compliance for phone answering means the service signs a Business Associate Agreement (BAA), encrypts call data, controls access to PHI, trains staff on HIPAA protocols, and has breach notification procedures. Not all AI answering services offer this.

A HIPAA-compliant answering service must:

  • Sign a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with your practice
  • Encrypt call recordings and transcripts in transit and at rest
  • Implement access controls limiting who can view PHI
  • Maintain audit logs of all access to patient information
  • Have breach notification procedures in place
  • Provide staff training on HIPAA protocols (for hybrid AI+human services)

The Smart Approach: Separate PHI and Non-PHI Calls

Most medical office calls don't involve PHI. Use an affordable AI receptionist ($99/mo) for scheduling, FAQs, and new patient intake, and route PHI calls to a HIPAA-compliant service. This can save $200-$500/month.

Calls that typically DON'T involve PHI (safe for any AI receptionist):

  • New patient inquiries and intake
  • Appointment scheduling and rescheduling
  • Office hours, directions, and parking
  • Insurance accepted questions
  • General service and pricing questions
  • New patient paperwork instructions

Calls that DO involve PHI (need HIPAA-compliant handling):

  • Test results or lab work discussions
  • Prescription refill requests with patient details
  • Detailed symptom discussions referencing patient records
  • Insurance claim or billing discussions with patient identifiers
  • Referral discussions mentioning patient conditions
